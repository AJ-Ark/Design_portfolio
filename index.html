Three Critical Failures

01

The Clarification Loop

Every project started with incomplete information. Demand managers spent 4-7 email exchanges (2-3 days each) asking follow-up questions. The root cause: forms that didn't adapt to context or expertise level.

“I spend more time asking ‘what do you need?’ than actually planning the work.”
— Portfolio Manager, Fortune 500
4-7 Email cycles per project
2-3 days Response time each
02

The Context Vacuum

Once clarified, demand managers manually searched 4-6 disconnected systems for similar projects, budget history, resource availability, and strategic alignment. This “context archaeology” took 12+ hours per project.

“I'm searching JIRA, Confluence, SAP, Workday, and Excel sheets just to understand what we've done before.”
— Demand Manager, Tech Services
4-6 Systems searched manually
12+ hrs Context gathering time
03

Approval Theater

Approvals took 5-10 days, but the actual decision took 5 minutes. The delay wasn't review time—it was attention management. Approvers were gatekeepers, not decision-makers, because they lacked context to make informed calls.

“By the time I approve it, half the details have changed. It's approval theater, not governance.”
— VP of Operations, Financial Services
5-10 days Approval bottleneck
5 min Actual decision time

Core Design Principles

No Forced Sequences

Users work in any order—Team-first, Milestones-first, or Parallel. The system never locks a tab because a previous step isn't “complete.”

Show Your Work

AI processing is never invisible. Progressive disclosure shows what the system is doing: fetching data, analyzing patterns, building recommendations.

Smart Defaults, Easy Overrides

AI suggests intelligent defaults based on historical data, but users can override anything. The system assists, never dictates.

Graceful Degradation

Draft state is valid. Incomplete work is saveable. No validation errors block progress. Support the messy middle of planning.

Conversation Ends, Work Begins

Tango conversation outputs to structured canvas immediately. No manual re-entry. 73% reduction in post-conversation data entry.

Honest reflection on what worked, what didn’t, and the skills forged through building an AI-powered enterprise product as a solo designer.

What Worked

01

Conversational UX Bet

Replacing forms with natural conversation was a risk that paid off. Users didn’t need training—they just talked. Completion rates jumped from 23% to 87%.

87% completion rate
02

AI Enrichment Transparency

Showing users exactly what the AI did—and letting them override it—built trust. The “show your work” approach turned skeptics into advocates.

81% AI suggestion acceptance
03

Adaptive Resource Architecture

Building the resource system to learn from organizational patterns meant recommendations got smarter with every project created.

+72 NPS score

What I’d Do Differently

01

Earlier Leadership Reviews

I waited too long to bring stakeholders into the design process. Earlier alignment would have prevented two rounds of rework on the governance model.

02

More Iterative User Testing

Testing in larger batches meant some usability issues compounded. Smaller, more frequent sessions would have caught issues earlier.

03

Better Documentation

As a solo designer, I under-invested in documenting design decisions. This made handoff harder and slowed onboarding of a second designer later.

LEARNINGS & INSIGHTS

For Designers Building AI Products

Key takeaways from designing an AI-powered enterprise tool—lessons that apply far beyond this project.

01

Show the AI’s Work

Users don’t trust black boxes. When AI enriches data, show exactly what changed and why. Let users override every suggestion. Transparency isn’t a feature—it’s the foundation of trust.

81% of AI suggestions were accepted when users could see the reasoning
02

Avoid One-Size-Fits-All

Enterprise users have wildly different mental models. An Engineering SVP thinks in sprints and resources. A Division Manager thinks in budgets and timelines. Design for the mental model, not the data model.

3 distinct personas required 3 completely different conversation flows
03

Governance ≠ Friction

The biggest design challenge wasn’t making things fast—it was making things fast while keeping compliance happy. Embed governance into the flow so it happens automatically, not as a gate.

Zero compliance violations while reducing process time by 99.8%
04

Conversation > Configuration

Every dropdown, checkbox, and form field is cognitive load. Natural conversation captures the same data with less friction. The best interface is the one users already know how to use—language.

87% completion rate vs 23% with traditional forms
